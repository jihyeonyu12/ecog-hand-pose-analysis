# Augmentation & Cross-Dataset Mixing — Final Report

**Date:** 2026-04-26
**Team:** g54, g.tec Spring School 2026
**Dataset:** ECoG Hand Pose (60 ch @ 1200 Hz, 4 classes: rest / fist / peace / open hand, n=179 trials)

---

## 1. Question

Can we improve g.tec 4-class gesture decoding by

1. **augmenting** the training set with synthetic ECoG segments, and/or
2. **mixing** with the open BCI Competition IV — Dataset 4 (Miller, finger flexion, 3 epilepsy patients)?

## 2. Headline result

| Approach | accuracy | balanced acc | macro-F1 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Baseline (TSM + Riemannian recentering, N_CH=48) | 0.715 ± 0.085 | 0.597 | 0.587 |
| **+ Miller fingers (1031 auxiliary trials)** | **0.754 ± 0.080** | **0.672** | **0.662** |
| Δ | **+3.9 pp** | **+7.5 pp** | **+7.6 pp** |

**75.4% accuracy, 67.2% balanced accuracy** on g.tec 4-class via Riemannian-aligned mixing with BCI-IV-D4. Augmentation gave **null result** on the same baseline.

---

## 3. Setup

- **g.tec data:** 60-channel ECoG @ 1200 Hz, 4 classes (rest / fist / peace / open hand). 30 trials per gesture + 89 rest segments → 179 trials.
- **Miller data:** BCI Competition IV-D4 (Kai J. Miller, U. Washington / Wadsworth, recorded at Harborview Hospital from epilepsy patients undergoing pre-surgical monitoring). 3 subjects: sub1=62 ch, sub2=48 ch, sub3=64 ch. 1000 Hz. Continuous recording with data-glove → individual finger flexion onsets → 1031 trials.
- **Pipeline:** trial covariance (with diagonal-loading shrinkage) → per-subject Karcher mean → recenter to identity (`C' = M^{-1/2} C M^{-1/2}`) → tangent-space at I → StandardScaler → Logistic-L2.
- **Validation:** 10-fold StratifiedKFold; preprocessing fit on train fold only; auxiliary Miller data added as training material only (never appears in test fold).

---

## 4. Part 1 — Signal-level augmentation (null result)

**Augmentations** (applied to raw bandpassed segments, train fold only, 2× synth per real trial):

- **jitter** — ±50 ms cyclic shift
- **psd_noise** — per-channel Gaussian noise scaled to 10–20 dB SNR
- **chan_drop** — zero out 3–10 % of channels
- **sig_mixup** — linear combination of two same-class trial segments, t ~ Beta(0.5)
- **combined** — jitter + psd_noise + chan_drop stacked

### Results (4-class, 10-fold CV)

| mode | acc | balanced acc | macro-F1 | Δ acc |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **none (baseline)** | **0.715** | 0.597 | 0.587 | — |
| jitter | 0.720 | 0.606 | 0.592 | +0.5 |
| psd_noise | 0.709 | 0.594 | 0.579 | −0.6 |
| sig_mixup | 0.704 | 0.581 | 0.566 | −1.1 |
| chan_drop | 0.687 | 0.556 | 0.544 | −2.8 |
| combined | 0.692 | 0.569 | 0.571 | −2.3 |

3-class results identical conclusion: best aug ≤ baseline (0.856 vs 0.856 for jitter; everything else hurts).

### Why it doesn't help

- **Trial covariance is shift-invariant** for stationary segments → jitter is mathematically a no-op for our pipeline.
- **Additive Gaussian noise** lowers SNR without adding any structure the classifier can exploit.
- **Channel dropout** removes the very spatial pattern the Riemannian metric uses to discriminate gestures.
- **sig_mixup** averages two raw signals → covariance averaging dampens the discriminative spectral peaks (high-gamma ERS, beta ERD).

Augmentation cannot manufacture inductive bias from 30 trials per class. The SPD manifold is already constrained by the small training set, and synthetic samples generated by simple signal transforms collapse onto the same manifold neighborhood without adding discriminative information.

We also re-validated existing **Riemannian mixup at the covariance level** (`pipeline_mixup.ipynb`, sweep over α ∈ {0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 1.0, 2.0}) — also flat: all variants stay at 0.83–0.84 vs baseline 0.844.

## 5. Part 2 — Cross-dataset mixing (works)

### 5.1 Why this is non-trivial

- **Channel counts differ:** g.tec=60, Miller sub1=62, sub2=48, sub3=64. Tangent-space dimension is k(k+1)/2, so vectors aren't directly compatible across subjects.
- **Electrode positions differ per patient** (clinical placement driven by suspected seizure focus, not standardized; no shared coordinate frame in the .mat distribution).
- **Sampling rates differ:** 1000 Hz (Miller) vs 1200 Hz (g.tec).
- **Label semantics differ:** Miller labels are individual finger flexions (1–5); g.tec labels are gesture poses (rest, fist, peace, open hand).

### 5.2 Approach

We use **per-subject Riemannian recentering** to identity. For each group (g.tec train fold, sub1, sub2, sub3 separately):

1. Truncate to the smallest channel count among the subjects in the mix (N_CH = 48, the size of sub2).
2. Compute trial covariances with diagonal-loading shrinkage.
3. Compute the **Karcher (Fréchet) mean** `M` on the SPD manifold using affine-invariant Riemannian metric.
4. **Recenter:** `C' = M^{-1/2} C M^{-1/2}`. After recentering, every group's covariance distribution is anchored at the identity I.
5. **Concatenate** g.tec-train + Miller covariances. Project to tangent space at I. The result is a feature space that is subject-invariant up to first order.
6. Train multinomial Logistic-L2 with **shifted Miller labels** (g.tec labels in {0..3}, Miller labels in {101..105}) so the classifier treats them as separate classes.
7. **At test time on g.tec validation fold,** restrict prediction to g.tec classes by looking only at decision-function columns corresponding to {0..3}.

### 5.3 Results

**Approach A — channel-invariant bandpower-stat features** (sanity check, not the main result):

| condition | acc | balanced acc | macro-F1 |
|---|---|---|---|
| g.tec only | 0.602 | 0.524 | 0.514 |
| + Miller fingers as aux classes | 0.618 | 0.543 | 0.535 |
| + Miller binary movement/rest as aux | 0.613 | 0.541 | 0.534 |

Small lift (+1.6 pp) but the underlying baseline is weaker than TSM (0.602 vs 0.715) because pooling-across-channels destroys spatial info.

**Approach B — Riemannian recentering, N_CH=48 (main result):**

| condition | acc | balanced acc | macro-F1 |
|---|---|---|---|
| g.tec only, recentered | 0.715 ± 0.085 | 0.597 | 0.586 |
| **+ Miller fingers (5 aux classes)** | **0.754 ± 0.080** | **0.672** | **0.662** |
| + Miller binary (move/rest) | 0.715 ± 0.078 | 0.625 | 0.597 |

**Ablations:**

| variant | acc | balanced acc | notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| N_CH=48, all 3 Miller subjects (1031 trials) | 0.754 | 0.672 | **best** |
| N_CH=60, sub1+sub3 only (711 trials) | 0.721 | 0.639 | more channels, less aux data → smaller gain |
| N_CH=48, Miller resampled to 1200 Hz | 0.743 | 0.655 | slight regression (resample ringing > PSD-mismatch cost) |

### 5.4 Where the gain concentrates

The improvement is **largest on balanced accuracy (+7.5 pp)** and **macro-F1 (+7.6 pp)**, not on raw accuracy (+3.9 pp). Reason: g.tec is class-imbalanced (rest=89, gestures=30 each), so plain accuracy is dominated by the rest class, which already worked. Miller's added structure most helps the under-represented active gesture classes — exactly where the BCI use case cares (Section "Metrics" in CLAUDE.md: "recall for must-detect gestures").

### 5.5 Why Miller helps despite different label semantics

g.tec and Miller share the same underlying neurophysiology: **sensorimotor cortex high-gamma ERS + beta ERD during voluntary movement**. After per-subject Riemannian recentering removes mean-cov bias, the additional 1031 Miller trials act as a **regularizer** for the LogReg classifier in tangent space — the shared "this is what motor activity looks like in ECoG" structure is reinforced even though the exact movement labels differ. The classifier ignores the aux-class outputs at test time but their gradient contribution shapes the shared tangent-space weights toward motor-relevant directions.

This is also why **finger-flexion (5 classes) outperforms binary movement/rest** as an auxiliary task: 5-way label structure carries finer-grained information about which channels and frequencies discriminate motor states.

## 6. Caveats

1. **Channel truncation is arbitrary spatially.** We took the first N channels per subject. Anatomical alignment via electrode coordinates would likely improve further, but coordinates are not in the BCI-IV-D4 .mat distribution.
2. **Sampling rates differ.** Miller @ 1000 Hz, g.tec @ 1200 Hz. We did not resample (resampling ablation showed it slightly hurts due to polyphase filter ringing). The covariance + tangent-space pipeline tolerates minor PSD-shape differences via shrinkage.
3. **Notch frequencies differ.** Miller (US) uses 60 Hz; g.tec may be 50 Hz (EU). Separate notch cascades are applied per dataset, but residual line-noise patterns could still differ.
4. **Test fold is g.tec-only.** Miller never appears in validation. Reported result is "auxiliary supervision improves g.tec generalization," not a transfer-learning claim about decoding Miller.
5. **No-leakage protocol:** Karcher means and StandardScaler are fit per CV fold on training data only. Auxiliary Miller data is constant across folds (we don't subsample it per fold).

## 8. Slide bullets (English, ready to paste)

> **Augmentation:** Five signal-level augmentations (jitter, PSD-noise, channel-drop, sig-mixup, combined) tested on TSM + LogReg baseline with 10-fold CV. **Null/negative result on n=179 trials.** Augmentation cannot substitute for inductive bias — covariance is shift-invariant (jitter is a no-op), additive noise lowers SNR without adding structure, channel dropout removes the very spatial pattern Riemannian methods exploit.
>
> **Cross-dataset mixing with BCI-IV-D4 (Miller, 3 epilepsy patients, 1031 finger-flexion trials):** Added as auxiliary classes after **per-subject Riemannian recentering to identity**. **+3.9 pp accuracy, +7.5 pp balanced accuracy, +7.6 pp macro-F1** on g.tec 4-class. Improvement concentrates in active gesture classes (where the BCI use case cares most) because Miller's sensorimotor covariance structure regularizes the tangent-space classifier toward motor-relevant directions, despite different label semantics.
